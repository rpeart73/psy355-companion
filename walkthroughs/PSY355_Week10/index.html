Week 10: Reflective Learning and Journaling
Fall 2026

Diary Writing Is Not Reflective Learning

A diary records what happened. Reflective learning does more: it moves from the event to what the event shows, and then to a next action. The same writing habit can stay at the level of memory or it can become learning, and the difference is the reflective step. Reflective writing is treated here as a resilience practice, not a private disclosure task.

Insight

A diary stores the event; a reflective journal turns the event into learning by naming what it shows and choosing what to try next. The reflective step is the difference.

Think about it

Think of a recent diary-style note you might write. What would change if you added the question: what does this show, and what will I try next?

Reflection Turns Experience Into Learning

Reflection is the work that turns experience into learning rather than memory alone. Recording an event keeps it. Reflecting on it asks what the event shows and what to do with that. The central question for the week is this difference, and the structured model gives reflection a shape so it does not stay at the surface.

Insight

Experience on its own is memory. Reflection asks what the experience shows and what to try next, which is how it becomes learning.

Think about it

When you look back on a difficult week, where do you usually stop, at what happened, or at what it shows and what you will try next?

A Structured Reflection Model

This week uses a short, structured reflection frame: what happened, what it shows, what I will try next. The model gives reflection a shape that moves from event to meaning to a concrete action. A structured model keeps the writing useful and prevents it from drifting into a personal disclosure task. The class path models a short reflection that moves from event to meaning to next action before students write their own.

Insight

Journaling and Critical Thinking

Reflective journaling does more than record learning; it can surface and develop critical thinking dispositions, the habits of mind a learner brings to a problem. Writing reflectively asks a student to question, weigh, and connect, which is why journaling and critical thinking are studied together. The disposition is not a grade or a speed; it is a habit that the journal can make visible.

Insight

A reflective journal can surface and develop critical thinking dispositions, the habits of mind a learner brings to a problem, not just a record of what happened.

Think about it

What habit of mind do you bring to a hard problem, and how might writing about it make that habit easier to see?

Reflection, Resilience, and Self-Regulation

Reflection connects to resilience and to self-regulation. Reflective writing is treated as a resilience practice, a way to make sense of pressure and recover, and it links to the self-regulated learning the course has already met, where students plan, monitor, adjust, and reflect. Naming the concept behind an experience is how reflection feeds the next plan. A personal experience can be connected to a course concept without oversharing.

Insight

Reflection is a resilience practice and part of self-regulation. Naming the concept behind an experience lets reflection feed the next plan, and it can be done without oversharing.
